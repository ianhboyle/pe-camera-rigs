## Overview

The VR180 Fisheye Camera Rig creates a stereoscopic 180-degree virtual reality camera setup that mimics professional VR180 cameras like the Canon RF 5.2mm F2.8 L Dual Fisheye lens. It's designed for the **easiest possible workflow** from Blender to YouTube VR with zero external programs required.

### What is VR180?

VR180 is YouTube's stereoscopic VR format that captures a 180¬∞ front-facing field of view with separate left and right eye images, providing 3D depth perception when viewed in a VR headset.

**Key Benefits:**
- Higher pixel density than 360¬∞ (better image quality)
- Captures what's in front of you (natural viewing)
- Smaller file sizes than full 360¬∞
- Perfect for narrative content, interviews, performances
- Native YouTube VR support

## Concepts & Understanding

### Interpupillary Distance (IPD)

The distance between human eyes, crucial for comfortable 3D viewing.

**Standard:** 64mm (average adult)
**Range:** 60-68mm (most comfortable)
**Rule of Thumb:**
- Closer objects ‚Üí smaller IPD (60mm)
- Distant objects ‚Üí standard IPD (64mm)
- Vast landscapes ‚Üí slightly larger (66-68mm)

**Why it matters:**
- Too large: Eye strain, "giant vision"
- Too small: Uncomfortable depth, "miniature vision"
- Just right: Natural, comfortable 3D

### Subject Distance (Sweet Spot: 3-15 feet)

**From Canon RF 5.2mm dual fisheye users:**
> "Subjects generally look best positioned between 3 and 15 feet from the camera - close enough to engage with but far enough to look pleasing"

**Distance Guidelines:**

| Distance | Effect | IPD Recommendation | Use Case |
|----------|--------|-------------------|----------|
| **<3 feet** | ‚ùå Excessive parallax, eye strain | 60-62mm | Avoid |
| **3-5 feet** | ‚úÖ Intimate, engaging | 62-63mm | Close-ups, interviews |
| **5-10 feet** | ‚úÖ Natural, comfortable | 64mm (standard) | General content |
| **10-15 feet** | ‚úÖ Environmental, spatial | 64-66mm | Performances, scenes |
| **>15 feet** | ‚ö†Ô∏è Depth less noticeable | 66-68mm | Landscapes (limited 3D) |

**Why This Matters:**
- **Too close:** Uncomfortable parallax, objects feel "in your face"
- **Sweet spot:** Natural depth perception, comfortable viewing
- **Too far:** Loses 3D effect, might as well be 2D

**Pro Tip:**
Place your main subject in the 5-10 foot range for optimal comfort. Use closer (3-5ft) for emphasis, farther (10-15ft) for environmental context.

### Field of View (FOV)

How much of the scene is captured.

**180¬∞ (Real):** What VR headsets display
**190¬∞ (Rendered):** Extra buffer for clean edges
**200¬∞ (Maximum):** Extreme wide-angle (rarely needed)

**Why 190¬∞ default:**
- Captures full 180¬∞ cleanly
- No edge vignetting
- Comfortable viewing
- Matches real VR180 cameras

### Fisheye Equisolid Projection

The lens model that matches real cameras.

**Equisolid** (Our default):
- Matches Canon RF 5.2mm lens
- Realistic distortion
- Accurate for VR

**Equidistant** (Not recommended):
- Circular fisheye
- Doesn't match real cameras
- Not good for VR180

## Best Practices

### Camera Placement

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Keep camera at eye level (1.6m)
- **Keep camera PERFECTLY level** (critical - no tilt!)
- Use static camera or slow movements
- Position rig at viewer's natural position
- Check horizon line is level in viewport

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Tilt camera even slightly (causes instant motion sickness)
- Fast pans or spins (nauseating)
- Handheld without stabilization
- Place camera at awkward heights

**‚ö†Ô∏è CRITICAL: Camera Leveling**

From Canon dual fisheye users:
> "Even a bubble level on a tripod may be more reliable than gyroscope sensors"

**Why It Matters:**
- Even **2¬∞ tilt** causes viewer discomfort
- Tilt is THE #1 cause of VR motion sickness
- Still images with tilt feel uncomfortable
- Cannot be fixed in post-processing

**Best Practice:**
- Ensure camera rotation X and Y = 0¬∞
- Only rotate on Z axis (panning) if needed
- Use visual horizon indicator in Blender viewport
- Add-on should warn if camera is tilted

### Content Design

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Design for 180¬∞ front view
- Keep important action in front
- Use depth to create interest
- Test in actual VR headset

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Put critical content behind viewer
- Use extreme close-ups (uncomfortable)
- Rapid cuts (jarring in VR)
- High-contrast flickering (headache-inducing)

### Lighting

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Even, soft lighting
- Conservative contrast
- Avoid blown highlights
- Use 3-point lighting preset

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Extreme highlights (very noticeable)
- Pure black shadows (detail loss)
- Flickering lights (headache)
- Strong backlight without fill

### Performance

‚úÖ **DO:**
- Use GPU rendering (OptiX/HIP)
- Enable denoising
- Start with Preview preset
- Test small frame range first

‚ùå **DON'T:**
- Render full animation without test
- Use CPU for final render
- Skip denoising (noisy VR is bad)
- Ignore render time estimates

## Troubleshooting

### Issue: Video not showing as VR180 on YouTube

**Symptoms:**
- Video plays as flat 2D
- No VR button in YouTube player

**Solutions:**
1. ‚úÖ Verify metadata was injected (check add-on log)
2. ‚úÖ Wait up to 1 hour after upload (YouTube processing)
3. ‚úÖ Re-render with "Verify Metadata" option enabled
4. ‚úÖ Manually inject using Google Spatial Media tool

### Issue: Stereo effect uncomfortable or wrong

**Symptoms:**
- Eye strain
- Double vision
- Depth feels wrong

**Solutions:**
1. ‚úÖ Check IPD is 64mm (or 60-68mm range)
2. ‚úÖ Verify cameras are at same height
3. ‚úÖ Ensure parallel convergence (not toed-in)
4. ‚úÖ Test with different IPD (start at 62mm for close scenes)

### Issue: Motion sickness reports

**Symptoms:**
- Viewers feel nauseous
- Dizziness
- Disorientation

**Solutions:**
1. ‚úÖ Ensure camera is level (no tilt)
2. ‚úÖ Reduce camera movement (or make static)
3. ‚úÖ Increase frame rate to 60fps
4. ‚úÖ Avoid fast pans and rotations

### Issue: Low quality / blurry in headset

**Symptoms:**
- Blurry when viewed in VR
- Pixelated
- Artifacts

**Solutions:**
1. ‚úÖ Increase resolution to 5760√ó2880 or higher
2. ‚úÖ Use Production or Final render preset
3. ‚úÖ Increase bitrate to 100Mbps+
4. ‚úÖ Check denoising isn't too aggressive
5. ‚úÖ Wait for YouTube to process high-quality version

### Issue: Render taking too long

**Symptoms:**
- Hours per frame
- Can't finish project

**Solutions:**
1. ‚úÖ Switch to GPU rendering
2. ‚úÖ Use Preview preset (256 samples)
3. ‚úÖ Reduce resolution for test renders
4. ‚úÖ Simplify scene complexity
5. ‚úÖ Render overnight or use render farm

## Color Spaces & Matching Real Footage

### Color Space Options

The add-on provides multiple color space presets for different workflows:

**sRGB (Default):**
- Best for: YouTube, web, general viewing
- Standard: Rec.709 color primaries
- Gamma: 2.2

**Canon Log 3:**
- Best for: Mixing CG with Canon camera footage
- Matches: Canon R5/R5C Log 3 output
- Wide dynamic range for color grading

**Rec.709:**
- Best for: Broadcast video, professional delivery
- Industry standard for HD video

**ACES:**
- Best for: High-end VFX, film workflows
- Maximum color accuracy and range

### Mixing CG with Real Canon Footage

If you're combining Blender renders with Canon dual fisheye footage:

1. **Set Blender to Canon Log 3** color space
2. Render your CG elements
3. Import both CG and real footage into editor
4. Color grade together in the same Log space
5. Export final video with Rec.709

This ensures perfect color matching between CG and real camera footage!

## Tips & Tricks

### Getting the Best Quality

1. **Resolution:** Higher is always better for VR (5760√ó2880 minimum)
2. **Frame Rate:** 60fps for smooth motion (90fps even better)
3. **Samples:** Don't skimp - 512 minimum, 1024 for final
4. **Denoising:** Use OptiX, but don't over-denoise (blurs stereo)
5. **Lighting:** Even lighting shows less artifacts

### Faster Workflows

1. **Preview First:** Always test 10-frame range before full render
2. **GPU Essential:** CPU rendering VR180 is painfully slow
3. **Proxy Renders:** Render low-res for timing, high-res for final
4. **Reuse Setups:** Save rig as asset library item
5. **Batch Render:** Queue multiple scenes overnight

### Creative Techniques

1. **Depth Layering:** Use foreground, midground, background
2. **Leading Lines:** Guide viewer's eye with composition
3. **Scale Reference:** Include human-scale objects for context
4. **Negative Space:** Give viewer's eyes places to rest
5. **Reveal Moments:** Let viewer discover details by looking around
